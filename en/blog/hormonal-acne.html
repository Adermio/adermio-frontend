Hormonal acne:
how to recognize & manage it

Chin, jawline, breakouts right before your period: learning to recognize hormonal acne, understand its causes, and know what to do to calm it.

May 21, 2026
8 min read

Breakouts that come back every month, always in the same spot, often right before your period. Skin that gave you trouble as a teenager and starts up again at 28 or 32. If this sounds familiar, there's a good chance your acne is hormonal. The good news: it's one of the best-understood forms of acne, and above all one of the most predictable — which makes it one of the easiest to manage once you've identified it.

What exactly is hormonal acne?

All acne is, to some degree, influenced by hormones. But we talk about hormonal acne when the dominant trigger is a hormonal fluctuation — most often linked to androgens, the hormones (like testosterone) present in women and men alike.

The mechanism is precise: androgens stimulate the sebaceous glands, which start producing more sebum. This excess sebum, combined with dead cells, clogs the follicle and creates the ideal ground for inflammation. What sets hormonal acne apart isn't a different mechanism from other types of acne, but its rhythm: it follows your body's hormonal fluctuations, especially the menstrual cycle.

How to recognize it: the 4 distinctive signs

No single sign is absolute proof on its own, but their combination is highly telling.

1. Location: the lower face

This is the most characteristic sign. Hormonal acne typically concentrates on the lower third of the face: the chin, the jaw (the well-known "jawline"), and sometimes the neck. If your teenage breakouts were mostly on your forehead and nose, and they now migrate downward, that's a strong clue.

2. Timing: cyclical breakouts

Hormonal acne is predictable. In women, it often intensifies in the 7 to 10 days before the period (luteal phase), when progesterone drops and the influence of androgens becomes relatively more pronounced. If you can almost "predict" your breakouts by your calendar, your acne is very likely hormonal.

3. Lesion type: deep and inflammatory

Unlike superficial blackheads and whiteheads, hormonal acne often produces deep, inflammatory lesions: tender red papules, and especially painful nodules or micro-cysts under the skin, which can take weeks to clear and easily leave marks.

4. The profile: adult acne in women

Hormonal acne largely affects adult women: it's estimated to concern around 40% of women between 25 and 40. It can appear for the first time in adulthood, even in someone who never had teenage acne. Men aren't spared, but for them the hormonal component is more stable, so cyclical breakouts are less obvious.

The most common hormonal causes

Behind hormonal acne there's rarely a "disease": most often, it's simply a physiological variation. Here are the most common situations.

The menstrual cycle

This is the most frequent and most benign cause. The drop in progesterone at the end of the cycle leaves the field open to androgens — hence the pre-menstrual breakout. It's not a disorder: it's a normal cycle your skin reacts to strongly.

Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)

PCOS is one of the hormonal causes to watch. It often comes with persistent, resistant acne, alongside other signs: irregular cycles, excess hair growth (hirsutism), difficulty losing weight. If your acne is accompanied by these symptoms, a medical check-up is strongly recommended — PCOS is managed very well once identified.

Changes in contraception

Starting, switching, or stopping a birth control pill can upset the skin's hormonal balance. Some pills improve acne, others worsen it, and stopping frequently triggers a temporary breakout lasting a few months, while the body finds its own balance again.

Pregnancy and postpartum

The hormonal upheavals of pregnancy and the period after birth often cause the skin to shift, one way or the other. Important: during pregnancy and breastfeeding, many common anti-acne actives are contraindicated. Never start a treatment during this period without the advice of a healthcare professional.

Chronic stress

Stress isn't a sex hormone, but it acts on the same machinery. The cortisol released during stress indirectly stimulates the sebaceous glands and amplifies inflammation. This is why hormonal acne almost always worsens during difficult periods.

Myths to set aside

"Hormonal acne = a serious imbalance"

False in the vast majority of cases. Most hormonal acne is linked to normal cycle variations, not a pathology. PCOS and endocrine disorders do exist, but they remain a minority — and they come with other signs that help spot them.

"You necessarily need a hormonal treatment"

No. Many cases of mild to moderate hormonal acne improve significantly with a suitable topical routine and a few lifestyle adjustments. Hormonal treatments (a specific pill, spironolactone) are useful in resistant forms, but they aren't the only path.

"It'll go away at menopause"

Not always — and above all: why wait years? Unmanaged hormonal acne gives marks and scars time to set in. Acting early mainly means limiting the aftermath.

Hormonal acne isn't a flaw in your body: it's skin reacting strongly to a perfectly normal rhythm. Understanding it is already the start of taking back control.

How to manage hormonal acne

Management rests on three levels, to be combined depending on severity.

1. A consistent topical routine

The basics stay the same as for any acne, but adapted to skin prone to deep inflammation:

  • Gentle cleansing morning and evening, without stripping (a cleanser that's too harsh worsens the rebound effect)
  • Targeted actives: salicylic acid (BHA) to unclog, niacinamide to regulate sebum and calm inflammation, retinoids to speed up cell turnover — outside of pregnancy and breastfeeding
  • Non-comedogenic moisturizing: well-hydrated skin regulates its sebum better
  • Daily sun protection, essential to keep marks from darkening

2. Lifestyle

Because hormonal acne is sensitive to your internal terrain, a few levers have a real impact:

  • Limit high-glycemic-index foods (fast sugars, ultra-processed products) that amplify insulin spikes
  • Moderate dairy products, especially skim milk, in people who are sensitive to it
  • Manage stress and protect quality sleep, two direct regulators of cortisol

3. Medical treatments

In resistant forms, a professional may offer more powerful treatments: prescription retinoids, hormonal treatments (certain pills, spironolactone), even isotretinoin in severe cases. These options are effective, but they're decided with a doctor, never through self-medication.

When should you consult?

Mild hormonal acne is very manageable at home. But some signals justify a medical opinion without delay:

  • Painful nodules or cysts that persist for several weeks
  • Scars forming (pitted or raised)
  • Acne alongside irregular cycles, excess hair growth, or weight gain — possible signs of PCOS
  • No improvement after 3 months of a well-followed routine
  • Significant psychological impact: anxiety, avoidance, loss of self-esteem

In summary

Hormonal acne is recognized by four clues: a low-face location (chin, jaw), a cyclical rhythm (pre-menstrual breakouts), deep, inflammatory lesions, and an adult-woman profile in most cases. Behind it there's most often a simple cycle variation — sometimes a PCOS to keep an eye on.

The good news is that this acne is predictable, so it can be managed. A consistent routine, a few lifestyle adjustments, and a medical opinion when needed are usually enough to get the upper hand. The first step is always the same: precisely identify what's happening on your skin, rather than treating blindly.
